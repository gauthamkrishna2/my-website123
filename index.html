The 2018 Kerala Floods: A Climate Story

It was a warm, muggy evening in August 2018 when Arjun stood by the window of his modest home in Alappuzha, Kerala. He looked out at the sky, which had darkened with ominous clouds. His wife, Meera, was busy putting their young daughter, Anya, to bed, but the growing intensity of the rainfall was already making her uneasy.

"I’ve never seen rain like this before, Arjun. Do you think we’ll be okay?" Meera asked, closing the windows tighter as the wind howled.

Arjun, a schoolteacher, had grown up in this house, witnessing monsoons year after year. Monsoons were the lifeblood of Kerala’s agricultural cycles, nourishing crops and refilling rivers. But this year felt different.

That night, as they huddled in their home, the rain didn't let up. In fact, it intensified, pounding relentlessly on the roof. The next morning, their village was unrecognizable. Streets had turned into rivers, and the lush green fields were submerged in brown water. The neighboring dam had released its waters, unable to contain the overflow.

A Conversation with Nature: Arjun Meets Dr. Isha

In the camp, Arjun crossed paths with Dr. Isha Nair, a climate scientist who had returned to Kerala to help with relief efforts. Amidst the chaos of distributing food and water, Arjun approached her with his question.

"Dr. Nair, you study climate change, don’t you? I don’t understand how things have gotten this bad. We’ve had monsoons all our lives, but this...this feels unnatural," Arjun said.

Dr. Isha sighed, looking at the swollen river that had taken over the village.

"I wish I could say it’s all a fluke, but the truth is, it’s not. The floods are a symptom of something much bigger, Arjun. The world is changing, and it’s because of us."

The Role of Greenhouse Gases

Dr. Isha explained patiently. "For decades, human activities—like burning fossil fuels, cutting down forests, and industrialization—have been pumping more and more greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. Gases like carbon dioxide and methane trap heat, causing global temperatures to rise. This warming affects the oceans too. The Indian Ocean, for example, is warming faster than other oceans."

Arjun looked thoughtful, watching the water lap against the walls of the relief center.

"And because the ocean is warmer," Dr. Isha continued, "there's more evaporation, more moisture in the air. This leads to heavier monsoon rains, like the ones we’ve been getting here. The rain was 42% higher than normal this year, and it overwhelmed everything—our rivers, our dams, even the land."

Meera, who had joined the conversation, asked, "But is this something we should expect every year now?"

Dr. Isha nodded slowly. "Unfortunately, yes. As greenhouse gases continue to rise, we’ll see more of these extreme weather events. The floods of 2018 are part of a pattern we’re seeing all over the world—stronger hurricanes, longer droughts, and heavier rains."

A Future at Risk

Back at the camp, Arjun felt a heavy weight settle in his chest. His family had been lucky; they were safe. But many others hadn’t been as fortunate. The devastation around him was a stark reminder that things were changing—faster than anyone had anticipated.

"But what can we do, Dr. Isha?" Arjun asked. "If this is all because of climate change, is there even a way to stop it?"

Dr. Isha smiled softly. "It’s not too late. We can reduce the amount of greenhouse gases we produce by switching to renewable energy, protecting our forests, and being smarter about how we use resources. Countries around the world are working on this, but it’s going to take a lot more effort. And locally, we need to improve our infrastructure—better dam management, better flood planning—to be more resilient."

As days passed, the floodwaters began to recede. Arjun, Meera, and little Anya returned to their home, but life wasn’t the same. Arjun’s understanding of the world had shifted. He knew that the Kerala floods were not just a natural disaster but part of a broader climate story—a story of how human activity was reshaping the very world they lived in.

Epilogue: A Call for Change

Months after the flood, Arjun found himself in his classroom, explaining climate change to his students, many of whom had lost their homes in the disaster.

"Climate change is like adding fuel to a fire," he told them. "It makes things that are already dangerous—like floods—much worse. But it’s something we can fight. The world has to come together to reduce greenhouse gases. We have to protect our future."
